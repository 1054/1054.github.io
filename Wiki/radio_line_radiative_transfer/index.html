Radio - Radio Line Radiative Transfer

Emission Intensity

Emission intensity is denoted as $ I_{\nu} $, in units of $ \mathrm{erg \, s^{-1} \, cm^{-2} \, Hz^{-1} \, sr^{-1}} $. The subscription $\nu$ means it is a function of photon frequency $\nu$. $\nu \equiv c / \lambda$, where $c$ is light speed, and $\lambda$ is photon wavelength.

Black body emission intensity denoted as $ B_{\nu} $ has a special form

$ \displaystyle B_{\nu} \equiv \frac{2 h \nu^3}{c^2} \frac{1}{e^{-{h \nu} / {k_B T}} -1} $

in units of $ \mathrm{erg \, s^{-1} \, cm^{-2} \, Hz^{-1} \, sr^{-1}} $. It is known as the Planck’s law (Max Planck 1900), a quantification of Kirchhoff’s law of thermal radiation.

Radiative Transfer

Matter can emit and absorb photon, i.e., kind of energy flux. When matter absorbs photons at its surface, its surface temperature increases. Meanwhile matter is always emitting photons at its surface following Planck’s law as long as its surface has certain temperature.

Here, let’s consider a cloud of hydrogen or dust grains as the “matter”. And we assume that matter is in an equiilbrium state, i.e., the energy it absorbs equals the energy it emits at the surface, and it has an equilibrium temperature T at its surface. And we assume its surface has same condition everywhere.

Besides, to some extent, we can consider these gas / dust clouds absorb energy in a form of $ \kappa_{\nu} \times I_{\nu} $ everywhere within the cloud, note that $ I_{\nu} $ changes here and there, but $ \kappa_{\nu} $ does not. Then we put these gas / dust clouds in our line of sight between us and some strong emission source, e.g., stars. These gas / dust clouds will absorb stellar photon and emit in their own way: both gas molecules/atoms and dust grains will emis black body radiation; then molecules/atoms will also have quantum transition emission/absorptions.

To measure how many energy being absorbed along the line of sight, we need to integrate the line of sight through the cloud:

$ \displaystyle \Delta I_{\nu} = I_{\nu,0} - I_{\nu} = \int_{\mathrm{cloud}} \kappa_{\nu} I_{\nu} \, \mathrm{d}{s} $

or in differential form $ \displaystyle \mathrm{d} I_{\nu} = \kappa_{\nu} I_{\nu} \, \mathrm{d}{s} $

or in logarithm form $ \displaystyle \mathrm{d} \ln I_{\nu} = \kappa_{\nu} \, \mathrm{d}{s} $, where $ \mathrm{d} \ln x = \mathrm{d} x \, \dfrac{1}{x} $.

so a solution is that $ \displaystyle I_{\nu} = I_{\nu,0} \, \mathrm{e}^{-\int \kappa_{\nu} \, \mathrm{d}{s}} $, $ \displaystyle \ln I_{\nu} = \ln I_{\nu,0} -\int \kappa_{\nu} \, \mathrm{d}{s} $.

So, from the stellar light’s entry point to the exit point, $I_{\nu}$ gradually decreases, but if we know the starting condition, $I_{\nu,0}$, then we can know the final condition $ \displaystyle I_{\nu} = I_{\nu,0} \, \mathrm{e}^{-\tau_{\nu}} $, where $ \displaystyle \tau_{\nu} \equiv \int \kappa_{\nu} \, \mathrm{d}{s} $ is usually used and known as the “optical depth”.

Dust

Here we continue to consider what happens to dust grains. Dust grains absorb stellar light and heat up to some dust temperature $T_\mathrm{d}$ and emit blackbody radiation. The absorption function is measured by our scientist in laboratories and $\kappa_{\nu}$ is proportional to $\nu^{\beta}$, where $\beta$ usually is around 1.8-2.0, for typical dust grains in star-forming galaxies (I may not be accurate here).
